# Azadirachta indica (Neem) — Comprehensive Knowledge Base

## Overview

Neem (*Azadirachta indica* A.Juss.) is a fast-growing, evergreen tree in the Meliaceae family, native to the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia. It is now naturalized pantropically across Africa, the Americas, Australia, and the Pacific Islands. Neem is one of the most researched medicinal plants, with over 300 identified phytochemical compounds and a multi-billion-dollar global market spanning biopesticides, personal care, pharmaceuticals, and nutraceuticals. Its seeds, leaves, bark, flowers, and fruit are all used therapeutically, with the seed kernel being the richest source of bioactive limonoids. The plant is heat-tolerant (up to 50 C), drought-resistant once established, and thrives on poor soils, making it a valuable agroforestry species for semi-arid regions.

---

## Botanical Identification and Taxonomy

*Azadirachta indica* was first described by Adrien-Henri de Jussieu in 1830. Its basionym is *Melia azadirachta* L. Synonyms include *Antelaea azadirachta*, *Melia indica*, and *Azadirachta indica* subsp. *vartakii* (uncertain status). The plant belongs to the order Sapindales, clade Rosids, within the angiosperms and eudicots. The NCBI Taxonomy ID is 124943.

The tree grows 15-20 m typically, reaching 35-40 m rarely, with a round dense crown 10-20 m in diameter. The trunk reaches up to 90 cm in diameter with dark grey bark that is reddish inside, deeply fissured, and produces a colorless sticky foetid sap. Leaves are alternate, simply pinnate, 20-40 cm long, with 8-19 ovate to lanceolate leaflets with serrate margins. Flowers are white to pale yellow, actinomorphic, arranged in axillary drooping panicles of 150-250 flowers, with a slightly sweet honey-like fragrance. The fruit is a 1-2 seeded drupe, ellipsoidal, 1-2 cm long, turning from green to yellow or purple when ripe, with a thin exocarp, pulpy fibrous mesocarp, and cartilaginous endocarp. Seeds are ovoid, 4000-4500 per kg, with a thin testa composed of shell and kernel. The root system features a deep taproot with extensive lateral roots.

Key differentiation from *Melia azedarach* (Chinaberry): A. indica has unipinnate leaves while M. azedarach has bipinnate leaves; A. indica flowers are white/cream versus lilac; and A. indica fruits are ellipsoidal yellow-green versus spherical yellow.

## Native Range and Distribution

Native to the Indian subcontinent (India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh) and Southeast Asia (Myanmar, Thailand, Indonesia, Malaysia), neem grows in mixed forests with *Acacia* spp. and *Dalbergia sissoo* in lowland monsoon forests at 0-1500 m elevation. It is now naturalized and cultivated pantropically across tropical and subtropical Africa, the Middle East (Saudi Arabia, Yemen), northern Australia, the Caribbean, Central and South America, and the Pacific Islands.

---

## Phenology

Flowering varies by region: February-May in India, year-round in Thailand, and restricted to the wet season in East Africa. Fruit development takes approximately 12 weeks from anthesis, with peak fruiting in India from May-August (peak June-July). Optimal harvest for seeds occurs when fruits turn from green to yellow-green, before full ripening and dispersal. Leaves can be harvested year-round, with highest bioactive content during active growth periods. First fruiting occurs at 4-5 years, with economical quantities at 10-12 years. The tree can live 150-200 years and remains productive for 40-50 years.

## Chemical Composition and Active Compounds

The primary bioactive class in neem is limonoids (tetranortriterpenoids), with over 300 phytochemicals identified across compound families including limonoids, flavonoids, phenolic acids, steroids, fatty acids, alkaloids, tannins, and carbohydrates.

### Key Bioactive Compounds

**Azadirachtin A** (C35H44O16, 720.71 g/mol) is the primary insecticidal compound with antifeedant, growth regulator, and ecdysone antagonist activity. It is most concentrated in seed kernels (3000-5419 ug/g dry weight), with lower levels in leaves (182-970 ug/g) and trace amounts in bark. Azadirachtin A is highly photolabile (half-life 48 minutes as UV thin film) and most stable at pH 4-6. Storage in amber bottles away from sunlight, moisture, and alkaline conditions is recommended.

**Nimbin** (C30H36O9) is anti-inflammatory, antiviral, and spermicidal, contributing to neem's bitter taste. It is found in bark, seed, and leaf.

**Salannin** acts as an insect repellent and antifeedant, present in moderate concentrations in seeds and leaves.

**Nimbidin** is a sulfur-containing limonoid with antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, anti-ulcer, and spermicidal properties, predominantly found in seed oil.

**Gedunin** exhibits anticancer, antimalarial, and anti-inflammatory activity, found in seed and bark.

**Nimbolide** has anticarcinogenic, antimicrobial, and anti-inflammatory effects, sourced from leaf and seed.

**Quercetin** and **Rutin** are flavonoid antioxidants with anti-inflammatory and antidiabetic properties, primarily found in leaves.

### Neem Oil Composition

Neem kernel contains 44-53% oil by weight. The fatty acid profile is predominantly oleic acid (~58%), stearic acid (~15%), palmitic acid (~14%), with trace amounts of linoleic, myristic, arachidic, and behenic acids.

### Standardization Markers

Primary quality control markers include Azadirachtin A (for biopesticides), Nimbin, Salannin, Quercetin, and Rutin (pharmacopoeial markers for leaf).

### Compound Stability

Neem compounds, particularly azadirachtins, are sensitive to light (rapid UV degradation), temperature (faster degradation above room temperature), pH (most stable at 4-6, rapid degradation in alkaline conditions), oxygen (accelerates photodegradation), and moisture. Commercial formulations like NeemAzal T/S are stable up to 24 months under recommended storage.

## Therapeutic Applications

### Traditional Medicine Systems

**Ayurveda**: Neem (Nimba) is first mentioned in Sanskrit texts around 1500 BCE, including the Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita, and Ashtanga Hridaya. Its Ayurvedic pharmacology is: Tikta (bitter) and Kashaya (astringent) tastes, Sheeta (cold) potency, Katu (pungent) post-digestive effect, and Laghu-Ruksha (light-dry) qualities. It pacifies Kapha and Pitta doshas. Key indications include Kushtha (skin diseases), Jwara (fever), Prameha (diabetes/metabolic disorders), Krimi (intestinal worms), Vrana (ulcers/wounds), and Kandu (itching). Classical formulations include Nimbadi Churna, Pancha Nimba Churna, Nimbamritasavam, and Arogyavardhini Vati.

**Siddha**: Known as Vemmu/Vembu, bark decoctions are used for skin eruptions.

**Unani**: Known as Azad-Darakht-e-Hindi, used for blood purification, skin diseases, and fevers. Classical preparation: Habb-e-Nimba.

### Modern Therapeutic Categories

**Antimicrobial**: Broad-spectrum activity against Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, viruses (including HCV NS3 protease inhibition), fungi (including *Aspergillus flavus*), and parasites (antimalarial, antileishmanial, anthelmintic). Key compounds: azadirachtin, nimbin, nimbidin, gedunin.

**Antidiabetic**: Multiple RCTs demonstrate hypoglycemic effects with reductions in PPBS, FBS, HbA1c, and insulin resistance. Neem is used as an adjunctive to metformin therapy. The best clinical evidence shows HbA1c reduction of 1.52% with 500 mg BID aqueous extract over 12 weeks.

**Anti-inflammatory**: Modulates NF-κB, TNF-α, and IL-6 pathways with dose-dependent edema suppression in animal models. Weaker than standard NSAIDs but with a broader safety margin.

**Anticancer/Chemopreventive**: In vitro activity against breast, cervical, colon, and prostate cancer cells via PI3K/Akt/mTOR inhibition and phase II detoxifying enzyme enhancement. Key compounds: nimbolide, gedunin, azadirachtin, quercetin. No published Phase II/III human trials exist for systemic cancers.

**Oral Health**: Anti-plaque and anti-gingivitis effects, reduction in periodontal pocket depth, active against *P. gingivalis*, *T. denticola*, and *T. forsythia*. Traditional use includes chewing sticks (datun).

**Dermatological**: Antiacne, anti-psoriatic, anti-eczema, wound healing, and antipruritic effects.

**Immunomodulatory**: Enhances macrophage phagocytosis, promotes M2 macrophage polarization for tissue repair, and suppresses the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype.

### Mechanisms of Action

Seven key mechanisms are documented: NF-κB pathway modulation (nimbolide, gedunin), PI3K/Akt/mTOR inhibition (nimbolide), Nrf2 activation (quercetin, catechins), MAPK pathway modulation (azadirachtin, gedunin), NS3 protease inhibition (3-deacetyl-3-cinnamoyl-azadirachtin) for HCV, mitochondrial uncoupling (neem oil components), and ecdysone mimicry (azadirachtin) for insecticidal action.

### Safety Profile

Oral LD50 for neem oil ranges from 14 mL/kg in rats to 31.95 g/kg in mice. Aqueous leaf extract LD50 exceeds 5000 mg/kg in rats. The estimated safe dose for azadirachtin is 15 mg/kg bw/day, for neem oil is 0.26 mg/kg bw/day, and for aqueous extracts is 0.3 mg/kg bw/day.

Serious adverse effects include pediatric encephalopathy/Reye-like syndrome (5-30 mL neem oil in infants), hepatotoxicity, metabolic acidosis, and bilateral vision loss in high-dose adult cases. Contraindications include pregnancy, lactation, children under 2 years, pre-existing liver disease, and partners planning conception.

## Cultivation and Agricultural Practices

### Climate Requirements

Neem thrives in tropical/subtropical climates with optimal temperatures of 21-32 C. It tolerates up to 50 C and is frost-sensitive, especially as seedlings. Annual rainfall of 400-1200 mm is optimal. Established trees have high drought tolerance due to a deep taproot but very low tolerance to waterlogging. Full sunlight is required for best performance, though partial shade is tolerated in the first 1-3 years.

### Soil Requirements

Optimal pH range is 6.2-7.0, tolerable from 5.0-8.5. Neem grows on sandy, sandy loam, laterite, clay loam, and black cotton soils, provided drainage is good. It tolerates dry stony, shallow, calcareous, saline, and alkaline soils but is intolerant of silty clays with slow drainage, sodic soils, and high or fluctuating water tables. Neem uniquely mines calcium from deep soil layers, neutralizing soil acidity via alkaline leaf litter.

### Propagation

Seeds are recalcitrant and rapidly lose viability. Fresh seeds have 70-90% germination within 5-14 days. Seed treatment involves removing pulp and soaking in water for 12-24 hours before sowing at 1-1.5 cm depth. Vegetative propagation is possible but not commercial-scale. Tissue culture methods include nodal segments, leaf explants, callus culture, and anther culture.

### Planting and Spacing

Planting season is the monsoon/rainy season (July-September in India). Standard block plantation spacing is 6 x 6 m (280 trees/ha), with wider 7 x 7 m to 10 x 10 m for agroforestry systems. Seedlings should be 4-12 months old and 15-45 cm tall at transplanting.

### Irrigation and Fertilizer

Irrigation every 5-10 days is needed for the first 1-2 years; mature trees require minimal to no irrigation. Neem has low nutrient requirements adapted to poor soils. At planting, apply 5 kg FYM + 25-50 g DAP per pit.

### Intercropping

Compatible crops include millet, sorghum, pulses, and other annuals. Deep taproot minimizes competition with shallow-rooted crops. Wider spacing (7x7 m or more) is required. Neem windbreaks increase crop yields by 15% or more.

### Pest and Disease Management

Common pests include tea mosquito bug (*Helopeltis* spp.), neem scale (*Pinnaspis strachani*), defoliating caterpillars, and termites. Diseases include powdery mildew (*Oidium* spp.), root rot (*Fusarium*, *Phytophthora* spp.), and leaf spot (*Cercospora*, *Colletotrichum* spp.). Organic control methods include 5% neem seed kernel extract, 2-3% neem oil spray, and azadirachtin-based commercial biopesticides.

### Harvesting

Seeds are harvested May-August in India when fruits turn from green to yellow-green. Initial yield at 5 years is 5-10 kg seeds/tree, reaching 35-58 kg at 20-25 years. Seed yield per hectare ranges from 5000-11750 kg depending on spacing and age. Post-harvest, seeds should be depulped, shade-dried for 3-7 days to 8-10% moisture, and processed within weeks due to recalcitrance. Oil extraction is done via mechanical cold-pressing, solvent extraction, or supercritical fluid extraction.

## Clinical Evidence

Neem has consistent preclinical evidence and several well-conducted RCTs in oral health and diabetes. The evidence base is strongest for type 2 diabetes mellitus, psoriasis, and oral health (plaque, gingivitis, periodontitis). No published Phase II/III results exist for systemic diseases at scale.

Key clinical trials include:
- Type 2 Diabetes: RCT with 80 patients, 500 mg BID aqueous extract over 12 weeks showed HbA1c reduction from 7.78% to 6.26%, PPBS reduction from 205.9 to 159.3 mg/dL, and improved endothelial function.
- Psoriasis: RCT with 50 patients showed significant PASI score reduction with neem extract 3 capsules/day for 12 weeks.
- Oral Health: Multiple RCTs demonstrate comparable anti-plaque efficacy between 2% neem mouthwash and 0.2% chlorhexidine.

## Regulatory Status

### India
Regulated as traditional medicine under AYUSH (Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940). Over 150+ ASU formulations containing neem are listed. Permitted as a botanical under FSSAI for health supplements with structure/function claims only.

### United States
Regulated as a dietary supplement under DSHEA 1994. Premarket approval not required. Neem oil fatty acids are considered GRAS; leaf/bark extracts are not established as GRAS.

### European Union
Some neem products marketed as food supplements; not systematically authorized as THMP. Neem extracts are not on the EU Novel Food list, requiring novel food authorization for food use.

### Conservation Status
IUCN status: Least Concern. CITES: Not listed (unrestricted international trade).

## Data Gaps and Future Research

Key data gaps include: no meta-analysis of neem for diabetes or oral health exists, no large-scale multi-center RCTs (n>200) for any indication, pharmacokinetic data in humans is minimal, most dermatological claims are extrapolated from in vitro data, and the regulatory status of neem in food supplements within the EU remains ambiguous and case-dependent. Recommended research priorities include a comprehensive multi-year azadirachtin variability study across global provenances, clinical validation of synergistic limonoid ratios, and assessment of climate change impacts on azadirachtin biosynthesis.
